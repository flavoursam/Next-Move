## Who these businesses are

Tourism operators sell experiences: guided tours, wildlife encounters, adventure activities, cultural experiences, boat trips, helicopter flights, wine trails, accommodation-linked activities, and attractions. Most are owner-operated small to mid-sized businesses in regional or tourist-destination locations.

In New Zealand and Australia, operators typically run between 1–5 staff in low season and scale up for peak. Revenue is highly seasonal — most operators see 60–80% of their annual bookings in a 3–5 month window. This creates genuine financial pressure around cash flow and occupancy.

**Common activity types:**
- Adventure and activity operators (rafting, bungy, skydive, kayak, dive)
- Wildlife and nature experiences (dolphin watching, whale watching, bird sanctuaries)
- Cultural and heritage attractions (Māori experiences, historic sites, museums)
- Food and wine experiences (wine tours, farm visits, cooking experiences)
- Accommodation-linked activities (lodge-based tours, glamping with guided experiences)
- Day tour operators (coach, boat, heli)
- Multi-day / overnight expedition operators

## FareHarbor — what we sell and why it matters

FareHarbor is a booking and business management platform built specifically for tourism operators. The core value proposition is direct booking revenue — replacing or reducing OTA dependency with a best-in-class direct booking flow embedded on the operator's own website.

**Key differentiators vs competitors (Rezdy, Checkfront, Bookeo, Bokun):**
- International distribution network — FareHarbor connects operators to global OTAs and resellers while also powering direct bookings from the same inventory
- No monthly fee model (in some markets) — operators pay per booking rather than a flat subscription
- Onboarding and migration support — we manage the transition, reducing the "too hard" objection
- Channel manager built in — one inventory source, distributed across multiple booking channels
- Built-in payment processing — reduces friction and consolidates reporting

## How they take bookings today

Many tourism operators use third-party OTAs (Online Travel Agents) like Viator, GetYourGuide, Expedia, and TripAdvisor Experiences as their primary booking channel. They pay 20–30% commission on every booking made through these platforms.

Booking software is common but fragmented. Rezdy, Checkfront, Fareharbor, and Bookeo are the most widely used. Many operators use one of these but still rely heavily on OTAs for volume, even though direct bookings on their own software generate significantly higher margin.

Operators with high TripAdvisor ratings and strong organic search visibility are often leaving significant direct booking revenue uncaptured — their reputation drives traffic, but the booking experience funnels visitors toward OTAs instead of directly.

## What they care about most

1. **Direct bookings** — reducing OTA dependency is the single most recurring conversation in this vertical. Every $100 booked through Viator costs them $20–30 in commission. The same booking through their own site costs near nothing.

2. **Peak season occupancy** — most operators run near capacity in peak season regardless of their booking system. The real question is whether they're capturing pre-arrival international bookings 4–8 weeks before departure, or relying on same-week walk-in and OTA traffic.

3. **Operational simplicity** — many operators are not technical. They do not want to learn a new complicated system. A tool that adds work without clear return will be rejected quickly.

4. **Low risk of disruption** — changing booking systems mid-season is terrifying to an operator. Conversations about switching are easier in the off-season or when a natural milestone exists (rebrand, new tour launch, ownership change).

## How to talk to them

**With owners:** Speak in revenue and margin terms. What does a 10% increase in direct bookings actually mean in dollars? Owners make gut-feel decisions — they respond to specific numbers over theoretical benefits.

**With general managers or operations managers:** Speak in workload and reliability terms. Will this reduce the admin burden? Will it work without constant maintenance? They care about what happens when something breaks.

**With marketing managers:** Speak in conversion and channel performance terms. How does direct compare to OTA conversion? What does the customer journey look like?

## Buying patterns and objections

- **"We're too busy right now"** — common in peak season. Note the timing and re-engage post-season. This is not a no, it is a timing signal. Set a wait and come back in April.
- **"We've looked at [competitor] before and it wasn't right for us"** — ask what specifically didn't work. This is often a CRM signal worth capturing. Different problem might have a different answer now.
- **"We're happy with Rezdy / Checkfront"** — this is rarely a closed door. It usually means "we haven't felt enough pain to change." The challenger play here is to show the gap between what they have and what's possible — usually direct booking share vs OTA share.
- **"Our main business is walk-in anyway"** — validate this. If true, it may genuinely reduce the value case. If it's 70% walk-in, there may still be a pre-booking and upsell story.
- **"We don't have the time to set it up"** — the objection is transition cost, not platform value. Address the onboarding support directly.
- **"We're locked into a contract with X"** — note the contract end date if possible and set a follow-up for 60 days before it ends.

## Signals that matter

**Positive signals:**
- High TripAdvisor rating (4.5+) + high review count (200+) = strong demand, established reputation, likely undermonetising direct channel
- Using Rezdy or Checkfront = known competitors, often underfeatured for international distribution
- No online booking widget visible on their own website = direct booking gap is real, not assumed
- Recent ownership change = natural reset moment, new owner may be open to change
- Existing OTA relationships (Viator badges, TripAdvisor booking button visible on their site) = commission leakage is quantifiable
- Previous demo or CRM conversation = they've shown intent before; lower education burden

**Negative signals:**
- Business listed as for sale or being sold = do not engage until ownership confirmed
- DNC flag on key contacts = route to another contact or flag for human review
- 90%+ walk-in business with no online presence = value case is thin
- Explicit "happy with current software" with no follow-up trigger = not the right moment
- No CRM activity in 6+ months with no context = cold; use a low-commitment re-engage

## Seasonal context for New Zealand

- **Peak season:** November – April (southern hemisphere summer)
- **Shoulder:** May, October
- **Off-season:** June – September
- **Best time for change conversations:** April–July (post-peak, before next season planning)
- **Worst time for system change conversations:** November–February (too busy, too risky)

**Regional variation:**
- Queenstown: extreme peak December–February, strong winter ski season (June–August). Ski-linked operators have an inverse peak — engage in Feb/March or September.
- Northland / Bay of Islands: peak aligns with school holidays and summer, slightly earlier (Oct–March)
- Milford Sound / Fiordland: more year-round due to international visitor mix, but still softer June–August

## Common buying triggers

These are events or realisations that tend to tip an operator from "not now" to "let's talk seriously":

- Seeing their OTA commission bill for the season for the first time and doing the maths
- A competitor switching to FareHarbor and visibly improving their direct booking flow
- A peak season where they sold out through OTAs and realised they left direct margin on the table
- New ownership — fresh eyes, no attachment to the existing setup
- A bad experience with their current software (downtime, poor support, missed bookings)
- A new product launch or rebrand where they're rebuilding the website anyway
