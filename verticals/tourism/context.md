# Tourism Vertical Context

## Who these businesses are

Tourism operators sell experiences: guided tours, wildlife encounters, adventure activities, cultural experiences, boat trips, helicopter flights, wine trails, accommodation-linked activities, and attractions. Most are owner-operated small to mid-sized businesses in regional or tourist-destination locations.

In New Zealand and Australia, operators typically run between 1–5 staff in low season and scale up for peak. Revenue is highly seasonal — most operators see 60–80% of their annual bookings in a 3–5 month window. This creates genuine financial pressure around cash flow and occupancy.

## How they take bookings today

Many tourism operators use third-party OTAs (Online Travel Agents) like Viator, GetYourGuide, Expedia, and TripAdvisor Experiences as their primary booking channels. They pay 20–30% commission on every booking made through these platforms.

Booking software is common but fragmented. Rezdy, Checkfront, Fareharbor, and Bookeo are the most widely used. Many operators use one of these but still rely heavily on OTAs for volume, even though direct bookings on their own software generate significantly higher margin.

Operators with high TripAdvisor ratings and strong organic search visibility are often leaving significant direct booking revenue uncaptured — their reputation drives traffic, but the booking experience funnels visitors toward OTAs instead of directly.

## What they care about most

1. **Direct bookings** — reducing OTA dependency is the single most recurring conversation in this vertical. Every $100 booked through Viator costs them $20–30 in commission. The same booking through their own site costs near nothing.

2. **Peak season occupancy** — most operators run near capacity in peak season regardless of their booking system. The real question is whether they're capturing pre-arrival international bookings 4–8 weeks before departure, or relying on same-week walk-in and OTA traffic.

3. **Operational simplicity** — many operators are not technical. They do not want to learn a new complicated system. A tool that adds work without clear return will be rejected quickly.

4. **Low risk of disruption** — changing booking systems mid-season is terrifying to an operator. Conversations about switching are easier in the off-season or when a natural milestone exists (rebrand, new tour launch, ownership change).

## How to talk to them

**With owners:** Speak in revenue and margin terms. What does a 10% increase in direct bookings actually mean in dollars? Owners make gut-feel decisions — they respond to specific numbers over theoretical benefits.

**With general managers or operations managers:** Speak in workload and reliability terms. Will this reduce the admin burden? Will it work without constant maintenance? They care about what happens when something breaks.

**With marketing managers:** Speak in conversion and channel performance terms. How does direct compare to OTA conversion? What does the customer journey look like?

## Buying patterns and objections

- "We're too busy right now" — common in peak season. Note the timing and re-engage post-season.
- "We've looked at [competitor] before and it wasn't right for us" — ask what specifically didn't work. This is often a CRM signal worth capturing.
- "We're happy with Rezdy / Checkfront" — this is rarely a closed door. It usually means "we haven't felt enough pain to change." The challenger play here is to show the gap between what they have and what's possible.
- "Our main business is walk-in anyway" — validate this. If true, it may genuinely reduce the value case. If it's 70% walk-in, there may still be a pre-booking and upsell story.

## Signals that matter

- High TripAdvisor rating + high review count = strong demand, established reputation, likely undermonetising direct channel
- Using Rezdy or Checkfront = known competitors, often underfeatured for international distribution
- No online booking widget visible on their own website = direct booking gap is real, not assumed
- Recent ownership change = natural reset moment, new owner may be open to change
- Business being sold = do not engage until new owner confirmed
- Heavy OTA dependency visible in marketing (Viator badges, TripAdvisor booking button) = explicit commission leakage signal

## Seasonal context for New Zealand

- Peak season: November – April (southern hemisphere summer)
- Shoulder: May, October
- Off-season: June – September
- Best time for change conversations: April–July (post-peak, before next season planning)
- Worst time for system change conversations: November–February (too busy, too risky)
