# Mid-Market Strategy

Use when: the deal involves multiple contacts or stakeholders, deal size is mid-range, the decision requires internal approval from more than one person, or the sales cycle is likely longer than a simple owner-decision.

The Mid-Market play requires earning trust across multiple people at different levels before a decision can move. ROI needs to be demonstrable, not implied.

## Core principles

**Multi-stakeholder awareness**
Identify who the champion is (wants the solution), who the decision maker is (signs off), and who might block (operations, finance, IT). Each person has a different concern. The pitch to an owner is not the same as the pitch to a GM.

**ROI over insight**
Unlike Challenger, which leads with a reframe, mid-market buyers often need to justify the decision internally. Make the ROI case concrete and easy to repeat in an internal conversation: "This pays for itself if it converts X% more of your existing web traffic."

**Proof points matter**
Reference similar businesses, outcomes, or case studies where you can. If you do not have specific case studies from the CRM, use general vertical benchmarks — but label them as benchmarks, not promises.

**Move toward a defined milestone**
Mid-market deals stall when there is no clear next milestone. Always be proposing the next concrete step: a second call with a stakeholder, a tailored proposal, a trial, a reference call.

## Angle guidance

The angle should focus on business outcome and be easy to pass up the chain — the champion needs to be able to summarise it to their boss in one sentence.

A good mid-market angle: "Operators your size who switch to direct booking typically recover the platform fee within 2–3 months through reduced OTA commissions — I can show you the calculation for your volume."

## Action guidance

- A well-prepared email with a specific ROI hook is often the right first move
- If you have multiple contacts, prioritise the champion first — get them on side before going wider
- The call to action should move toward a meeting where you can present a tailored case, not just a casual reply
- Avoid short, casual outreach — mid-market buyers expect a degree of preparation

## Draft tone

Professional, specific, outcome-focused. Slightly more formal than Challenger. Respects that the reader has internal hurdles to clear and needs ammunition to do so.

Example tone: "For a business at your scale — based on your TripAdvisor volume and current software — I'd estimate you're leaving $30–50k in direct booking revenue on the table annually through OTA fees alone. I've put together a rough breakdown based on similar NZ operators. Worth 20 minutes to see if the numbers make sense for you?"

## Disqualification rule

If there is no identifiable champion or the deal size does not justify the multi-stakeholder sales cycle, step back to Discovery or Challenger. Do not run a mid-market playbook on a small owner-operator.
